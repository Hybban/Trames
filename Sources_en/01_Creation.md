# I. Character Creation

## 1 The Campaign Promise
Before creating your characters, take a moment to define the intention of your campaign together. Not the plot — that will be the business of the first session — but what you want to *explore* or *feel* while playing.

The Game Master asks the question out loud. Each player answers in one or two sentences. Then the table finds a common formulation that reflects the whole.

> *Examples:*
> *"We want to explore what it costs to choose between loyalty and survival."*
> *"We want an epic adventure where every victory comes at a price."*
> *"We want to tell the story of a group rebuilding itself after a foundational failure."*

This promise is not a rigid contract. It can evolve over the sessions. But it serves as a compass when narrative choices are difficult — and a reminder, at the end of the campaign, of what the Moirai had woven from the beginning.

Write it down somewhere where everyone can see it.

## 2. Defining Traits
A hero is made up of a maximum of **eight traits** at the start. The character's number of traits may increase as their adventures progress. Although you can choose them freely, favor short and precise phrasing without being too restrictive. A trait can be an asset, but also a source of narrative complication (e.g., "Curious", "Devoted to their family").

Distribute your eight traits according to the following structure:

* **The Archetype (1 trait):** It defines your primary role or essence (e.g., "Battle Mage", "Repentant Thief").
* **Behavior (2 to 3 traits):** They describe your personality or your way of acting (e.g., "Natural Diplomat", "Suspicious of authority").
* **Abilities & Talents (the rest):** Your technical skills, powers, or special gifts (e.g., "Master Archer", "Keen senses").

## 3. Assigning Scores
Each trait has a die value that represents its maximum potential. At creation, you have a pool of dice to distribute among your eight traits:

* **1 trait at d12** (Reserved for the Archetype at character creation).
* **2 traits at d10**.
* **2 traits at d8**.
* **3 traits at d6**.

The value given to this trait is its current maximum value. This maximum value can evolve positively as adventures and tests progress. During play, the trait's value will fluctuate between its maximum value and d6.

> **Note on fluid creation:** You are not obliged to define all eight of your traits immediately. You can leave some "blank" and name them during play to meet a narrative need, drawing from the remaining dice in your initial pool.

## 4. Resources
In addition to their traits, a hero possesses between **1 and 5 resources** (unique items, contacts, social title, mount).

* **Narrative Use:** A resource can make an action automatically succeed if the situation allows (e.g., using a "Skeleton Key" for an ordinary lock).
* **Mechanical Use:** If you use a resource during a dice roll, you can **double one of the trait dice rolled** (roll the die twice and keep the better result before comparing it to the other traits).
* **Accessibility:** A resource can become inaccessible following a failure or complication.

> **Note on resources:** Resources are defined by the player, but must be validated by the Game Master to ensure they are consistent with the universe and the scenario. Also, just like for traits, it is possible to define resources during play according to narrative needs and respecting the limit of 5.

## 5. The Loom of the Fates: The Three Threads
In Greek mythology, the Moirai are the goddesses of fate. There are three of them: Clotho, Lachesis, and Atropos. They spin, measure, and cut the thread of life of every mortal. THREADS uses this imagery to define the hero's destiny.

At creation, you define three narrative statements that represent the soul of your character. These are your Threads — and they are not just fiction. They interact directly with the token pool of the Loom, the engine of dramatic energy in the game.

**The Loom's Pool**: At the start of a session, the Game Master places a number of tokens equal to the number of players + 1 in the center of the table. This pool belongs to the players initially, but can quickly end up in the hands of the Game Master as Threads are invoked.

### Clotho's Thread (Conviction)
A sentence describing what your hero believes in or their moral compass. It's what pushes them to go beyond their limits.

* **Clotho spins the thread.** She creates, she gives momentum. To act from one's Conviction is to draw from one's own reserves, to burn something of oneself to move forward. It costs a token because faith is spent. When your action is driven by this deep Conviction, you can spend 1 token from the Loom to add +1d12 to your roll. The token is given to the Game Master. Clotho spins — she costs you something, because creating requires effort.

### Lachesis's Thread (Destiny)
A prophecy, a quest, or an ultimate goal. It's the direction their life is taking.

* **Lachesis measures the thread.** She does not create, she recognizes. When Destiny is fulfilled, it is not the hero forcing it — it is the universe aligning. If you act directly to fulfill your Destiny, add +1d12 to your roll. This use is free — Lachesis measures, she recognizes. But beware, the moments when Destiny is fulfilled are rare: only once per session.

### Atropos's Thread (Sin)
A vice, a weakness, or a dark instinct. It's what will cause their downfall or make them fall.

* **Atropos cuts the thread.** She is the most feared of the three. To give in to Sin is to invite her to approach your weave. If you let Atropos guide your hand — if you give in to your vice or your dark instincts — you regain 1 token taken from the Game Master. In return, add +1d4 to your current roll. This die cannot help you, but it can complicate everything. Atropos cuts — she invigorates you while fraying your thread.

> **Note on Threads:** Threads are defined by the player, but must be validated by the Game Master, and the other players if necessary, to ensure they are consistent with the universe and the scenario. It is also possible to define Threads during play according to narrative needs, but beware, this should not be done lightly.

## 6. The Campaign Promise
Before creating your characters, take a moment to define the intention of your campaign together. Not the plot — that will be the business of the first session — but what you want to explore or feel while playing. You are weaving a common tapestry together.

The Game Master asks the question out loud. Each player answers in one or two sentences. Then the table finds a common formulation that reflects the whole.

Examples:

* "We want to explore what it costs to choose between loyalty and survival."
* "We want an epic adventure where every victory comes at a price."
* "We want to tell the story of a group rebuilding itself after a foundational failure."

This promise is not a rigid contract. It can evolve over the sessions. But it serves as a baseline Thread when narrative choices are difficult — and a reminder, at the end of the campaign, of what the Moirai had woven from the beginning. Write it down somewhere where everyone can see it. Remember that this is your story, and that you have the power to shape it together.
