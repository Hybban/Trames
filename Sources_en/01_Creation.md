# I. Character Creation

## 1 Session Zero
Before creating your characters, take a moment to discuss and define the intent of your campaign together. Not the plot—that will be the focus of the first session—but what you want to *explore* or *experience* while playing.

The Game Master asks the question aloud. Each player responds with one or two sentences. Then the group comes up with a shared statement that reflects the whole.

> *Examples:*
> *"We want to explore the cost of choosing between loyalty and survival."*
> *"We want an epic adventure where every victory comes at a price."*
> *"We want to tell the story of a group rebuilding itself after a founding failure."*

This promise isn’t a rigid contract. It can evolve over the course of the sessions. But it serves as a compass when narrative choices are difficult—and as a reminder, at the end of the campaign, of what the Fates had woven from the beginning. This is done, of course, with the emotional well-being of all players at the table in mind.

Write it down somewhere where everyone can see it.

## 2. Defining Traits
A hero starts with a maximum of **eight traits**. The number of traits a character has may increase (and even decrease under certain circumstances) as their adventures unfold. Although you are free to choose them, opt for short, precise phrasing without being too restrictive. A trait can be just as much an asset as a source of narrative complication (e.g., “Curious,” “Devoted to Family”).

Distribute your eight traits according to the following structure:

* **The Archetype (1 trait):** This defines your main role or essence (e.g., "Battle Mage," "Repentant Thief").
* **Behavior (2 to 3 traits):** These describe your personality, your way of acting, and your demeanor (e.g., “Natural Diplomat,” “Distrustful of Authority”).
* **Abilities & Talents (the rest):** Your technical skills, special powers or gifts, and other expertise (e.g., "Master Archer," "Keen Senses").

## 3. Assigning Scores
Each trait has a die value that represents its maximum potential. Upon character creation, you have a pool of dice to distribute among your eight traits:

* **1 trait with a d12** (Reserved for the Archetype upon character creation).
* **2 traits with d10**.
* **2 traits with d8**.
* **3 traits with d6**.

The value assigned to this trait is its current maximum value. This maximum value may change over the course of adventures and tests. During the game, the trait’s value will fluctuate between its maximum value and d6.

> **Note on fluid character creation:** You are not required to define all eight of your traits immediately. You can leave some "empty" and name them during the game to meet a narrative need, drawing from the remaining dice in your initial reserve.

## 4. Resources
In addition to their traits, a hero possesses between **1 and 5 resources** (unique items, contacts, social status, mount).

* **Narrative Use:** A resource can automatically succeed an action if the situation calls for it (e.g., using a “Master Key” on an ordinary lock).
* **Mechanical Use:** If you use a resource during a dice roll, you can **double one of the trait dice rolled** (roll the die twice and keep the best result before comparing it to the other traits). The chosen trait must be related to the resource used. For example, if your character owns a **horse** (resource), it makes sense to double the die for the **Horseback Riding** skill. It does not make sense to double the die for the **Magic** skill, unless the horse is magical.
* **Accessibility:** A resource may become inaccessible following a failure or a complication.

> **Note on resources:** Resources are defined by the player, but must be approved by the Game Master to ensure they are consistent with the setting and the scenario. Also, just as with traits, it is possible to define resources during the game as narrative needs arise, while respecting the limit of 5.

## 5. The Loom of the Moirai: The Three Threads
In Greek mythology, the Moirai are the goddesses of fate. There are three of them: Clotho, Lachesis, and Atropos. They spin, measure, and cut the thread of every mortal’s life. THREADS draws on this imagery to define the hero’s destiny. 

At character creation, you define three narrative statements that represent your character’s soul. These are your Threads—and they are not merely fiction. They interact directly with the Weaving Loom token reserve, the game’s engine of dramatic energy.

**The Loom’s Reserve**: At the start of the session, the Game Master places a number of tokens equal to the number of players + 1 in the center of the table. This reserve belongs to the players at the start, but can quickly end up in the Game Master’s hands as the Threads are invoked.

### Clotho’s Thread (Conviction)
A sentence describing what your hero believes in or their moral compass. This is what drives them to push themselves. 

* **Clotho spins the thread.** She creates; she provides momentum. Acting from your Conviction means drawing on your own reserves, burning a part of yourself to move forward. It costs a token because faith takes a toll. When your action is driven by this deep Conviction, you may spend 1 Loom token to add +1d12 to your roll. The token is given to the Game Master. Clotho spins—she costs you something, because creation requires effort.

### The Thread of Lachesis (Destiny)
A prophecy, a quest, or an ultimate goal. It is the direction your life takes.

* **Lachesis measures the thread.** She does not create; she recognizes. When Destiny is fulfilled, it is not the hero who forces it—it is the universe that aligns. If you act directly to fulfill your Destiny, add +1d12 to your roll. This use is free—Lachesis measures, she recognizes. But be careful, moments when Destiny is fulfilled are rare: only once per session.

### The Thread of Atropos (Sin)
A vice, a weakness, or a dark instinct. This is what will cause their downfall or lead them to fall.

* **Atropos cuts the thread.** She is the most feared of the three. To give in to Sin is to invite her to approach your thread. If you let Atropos guide your hand—if you give in to your vice or your dark instincts—you regain 1 token taken from the Game Master. In exchange, add +1d4 to your current roll. This die cannot help you, but it can complicate everything. Atropos cuts—she invigorates you while fraying your thread.

> **Note on Threads:** Threads are defined by the player, but must be approved by the Game Master, and the other players if necessary, to ensure they are consistent with the universe and the scenario. It is also possible to define Threads during the game as narrative needs arise, but be careful—this should not be done lightly.

## 6. The Thickness of the Thread
Before the first scene, the Fates have already chosen the thickness of your thread. Some heroes are woven to last—they take hits, they bounce back; their weave is dense and resilient. Others are fine threads, stretched to the limit, where every knot could be the last. The Game Master chooses the thread gauge to match the campaign’s tone.

* **Linen Thread:** The heroes are mortal. Every wound matters. The story is dark, and the scars are permanent.
* **Woolen Thread:** The THREADS standard. The heroes are sturdy but vulnerable. Losses carry weight.
* **Silken Thread:** The heroes are cut out for adventure. They fall, but they get back up. Epic triumphs over tragedy.
* **Golden Thread:** Heroes are legendary. Fate protects them as much as it tests them. The Fates themselves are watching.

These four game modes affect certain aspects of the game mechanics, described in the following chapters.
