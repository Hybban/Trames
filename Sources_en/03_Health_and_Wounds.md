# III. Thread Solidity

In THREADS, the Moirai do not distinguish what wears down your thread. A blade, a humiliation, a sleepless night — everything accumulates on the same weave. What matters is the state of the thread, not the nature of the wound.

It is up to the Game Master and the players to name what each ticked box represents. A serious wound could be a broken leg, a destroyed reputation, or a shaken conviction. The mechanic is the same. The fiction, however, must be precise.

## 1. Damage Tiers
Damage accumulates according to its severity. As long as the damage is light, there is no mechanical impact, but it acts as a buffer before more critical states.

* **Frayed Thread (3 notches):** Wounds are light and superficial, no immediate mechanical impact. If you take damage while these boxes are full, it spills over into serious damage.
* **Taut Thread (3 notches):** Wounds are serious and crippling, but the character can still hold on. You add **1d4** to all your dice rolls.
* **Thread at the Limit (3 notches):** Wounds are severe and the character is on the verge of breaking. You add **3d4** to all your dice rolls.

> **The "Tangled Threads" Effect:** The d4s added by wounds are damaged threads that tangle in your action. They cannot roll higher than a 4 and thus provide no possibility of success, even partial. Their sole effect is to increase the probability of a snag — triggering complications and the degradation of your resources. They mechanically represent what pain and fatigue do to a thread: encumber it without strengthening it.

## 2. Taking Damage
Damage is often taken as a result of a dice roll:

* **Total Success (9 – 12):** The character avoids the worst. Damage is non-existent, or perhaps light in some very critical situations.
* **Success with a Cost (5 – 8):** The character potentially takes damage. This can be offset by narrative complications instead of damage. If no narrative complication is desired, then the character takes light damage. But it can be more severe if the situation calls for it.
* **Failure (1 – 4):** The character is fully hit. The consequences are severe and immediate. Depending on the context, this could be physical, psychological, or social damage.

## 3. Recovery and Healing
The time and resources required to heal depend on the depth of the injuries:

* **Light Recovery:** All light wound boxes are automatically cleared at the **end of the scene**.
* **Deep Recovery:** To clear **Serious** or **Mortal** wound boxes, a simple pause is not enough. The hero must engage in a dedicated resting scene and justify adequate support (medical care, therapy, a sanctuary, or help from a specialized ally).

> **Note on narrative recovery:** Boxes are recovered mechanically according to the usual rules. But a social wound cannot be healed by a doctor, and a mental trauma does not disappear after a good night's sleep. The Game Master will ensure that the fiction of the recovery is consistent with the fiction of the wound.

### The Caliber of the Thread
Before the first scene, the Moirai have already chosen the thickness of your thread. Some heroes are woven to last — they endure, they bounce back, their weave is dense and resilient. Others are thin threads, stretched to the extreme, where every knot could be the last. The Game Master chooses the caliber of the thread in accordance with the tone of the campaign.

* **Linen Thread:** The heroes are mortal. Every wound counts. The story is dark and the scars, permanent.
* **Wool Thread:** The standard for THREADS. The heroes are solid but vulnerable. Losses carry weight.
* **Silk Thread:** The heroes are built for adventure. They fall, but they get back up. The epic takes precedence over tragedy.
* **Golden Thread:** The heroes are legendary. Destiny protects them as much as it tests them. The Moirai themselves are watching.
