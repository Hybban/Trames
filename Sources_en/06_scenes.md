# VI. STRUCTURING THE FRAMEWORK: MANAGING SCENES

In **FRAMEWORKS**, the story does not flow like a continuous stream, but is woven together through successive sequences called “scenes.” Just like in theater or film, a scene is a unit of time, place, and action that helps set the pace of the game and triggers the system’s recovery mechanisms.

## 1. What Is a Scene?

A scene begins when the heroes are confronted with a situation involving clear stakes (a tense negotiation, a dangerous exploration, a fight, an investigation at a crime scene). It ends naturally when the main issue is resolved, the heroes change locations, or a significant time jump occurs.

The end of a scene marks a pause in the narrative, a moment when the dust settles before the Fates pull the strings again.

## 2. The Economy of the Scene (Mechanical Impact)

The end of a scene is not just a narrative transition; it is a central mechanism of the heroes’ attrition and survival. When a scene ends, apply the following effects:
* **Light Recovery:** All *Frayed Thread* injury boxes are automatically cleared.

* **Traits Restored:** If a trait (Behavior or Ability) had dropped below d6 and become unusable, the change in narrative context of the new scene allows it to become active again.

* **Breath Restored:** If the character sustained only frayed injuries during the scene, they also recover all their traits to their maximum level to begin the next scene.

## 3. Rest Scenes

To heal from more serious aftereffects (*Tightrope* or *On the Edge*), the natural transition from one scene to the next is not enough. Players must explicitly request and frame a **Rest Scene**.

* **Justification:** The hero must demonstrate adequate support to trigger this deep recovery: medical care, therapy, returning to a sanctuary, using a specific resource, or help from an ally.

* **Narrative Consistency:** The recovery must make sense. Psychological trauma cannot be healed with bandages, and a ruined reputation (social injury) does not disappear after a good night’s sleep. The Game Master and the players ensure that the fiction of the healing aligns with the fiction of the injury.

## 4. Framing and Closing (Tips for the Game Master)

Mastering the pace of a game relies on two simple actions: framing (opening) and cutting (closing) a scene.

* **Framing the scene:** Don’t start with the tedious journey; start with the action or the tension. *“You arrive at the temple gates, but two heavily armed guards are blocking your path. What do you do?”*
* **Cutting the scene:** As soon as the dramatic conflict is resolved (the fight is won, the crucial information is obtained), don’t let the situation drag on unnecessarily. Announce the end of the scene. This adds dynamism to your game and allows the players to immediately use their *Light Recovery*.
