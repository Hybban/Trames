# VI. STRUCTURING THE THREADS: MANAGING SCENES

In **THREADS**, the story does not flow like a continuous stream, but is woven together through successive sequences called "scenes." Just like in theater or film, a scene is a unit of time, place, and action that helps set the pace of the game and triggers the system's recovery mechanisms.

## 1. What is a Scene?

A scene begins when the heroes are confronted with a situation involving clear stakes (a tense negotiation, a dangerous exploration, a fight, an investigation at a crime scene). It ends naturally when the main issue is resolved, the heroes change locations, or a significant time jump occurs.

The end of a scene marks a pause in the narrative, a moment when the dust settles before the Moirai pull the strings again.

## 2. The Economy of the Scene (Mechanical Impact)

The end of a scene is not just a narrative transition; it is a central mechanism of the heroes' attrition and survival. When a scene ends, apply the following effects:

* **Light Recovery:** All *Frayed Thread* injury boxes are automatically cleared.

* **Traits Restored:** If a trait (Behavior or Ability) had dropped below d6 and become unusable, the change in narrative context of the new scene allows it to become active again at d6.

* **Breath Restored:** If the character sustained only frayed injuries during the scene, they also recover all their traits to their maximum level to begin the next scene. With the exception, of course, of the traits that have just been restored.

## 3. Rest Scenes

To heal from more serious aftereffects (*Taut Thread* or *Thread at Breaking Point*), the natural transition from one scene to the next is not enough. Players must explicitly request and frame a **Rest Scene**.

* **Justification:** The hero must demonstrate adequate support to trigger this deep recovery: medical care, therapy, returning to a sanctuary, using a specific resource, or help from an ally.

* **Narrative Consistency:** The recovery must make sense. Psychological trauma cannot be healed with bandages, and a ruined reputation (social injury) does not disappear after a good night's sleep. The Game Master and the players ensure that the fiction of the healing aligns with the fiction of the injury.

**Examples of Rest Scenes:**

* **Medical Care Scene:** *"Your wound is still bleeding, but you manage to reach a hospital. A dedicated nurse stitches you up and administers painkillers."*
* **Psychological Support Scene:** *"After the confrontation, you need to talk. You meet with your therapist, who helps you put your feelings into words."*
* **Spiritual Healing Scene:** *"The temple offers a peaceful refuge. You spend the night in prayer, regaining your serenity."*

It is important to note that Rest Scenes are not automatic, even if the context is suitable. Players must request them and justify them through the narrative of adequate support. Furthermore, the narrative context must be consistent with the injury. For example, a severe physical injury cannot be treated in the context of a frantic escape, and a psychological injury cannot be healed without dialogue or appropriate support.

And the advancement of clocks must be taken into account. Indeed, the rest of the world evolves while the players decide to take a well-deserved rest for their characters.

## 4. Framing and Closing (Tips for the Game Master)

Mastering the pace of a game relies on two simple actions: framing (opening) and cutting (closing) a scene.

### A. Framing a scene

Avoid laborious introductions. Dive straight into the heart of the matter by presenting the situation with tension. For example, instead of describing the journey to the location of the action, start directly with the arrival at the scene: *"You arrive at the temple gates, but two heavily armed guards are blocking your path. What do you do?"*

This is also true for starting a scenario. Starting *In Medias Res* allows players to immediately be in the action, understand the situation, and act accordingly. Flashbacks can always be used later to explain how the characters got there. These can explain certain elements of the past and justify certain traits, if they haven't all been defined at the start, or to make certain scenes more consistent with the character's history.

Do not describe boring journeys. Use narrative ellipses to move from one place to another without losing momentum.

### B. Cutting a scene

Identify the exact moment when the main stakes of the scene are resolved (victory, critical failure, crucial information obtained).

Do not let the situation drag on. Once the objective is reached, conclude the scene without delay.

The end of a scene is the ideal time to apply *Light Recovery*, offering a breath of fresh air to the characters before the next sequence.

## 5. Types of scenes 

There are several types of scenes in THREADS:

* **Action scenes:** scenes where characters face physical danger or a tense situation.
* **Investigation scenes:** scenes where characters seek to solve a riddle or uncover the truth.
* **Interaction scenes:** scenes where characters interact with other characters, whether allies or enemies.
* **Rest scenes:** scenes where characters rest and recover from their injuries.
* **Flashback scenes:** scenes where characters recall past events. 
* **Exploration scenes:** scenes where characters discover a new environment, traverse hostile territories, or map unknown places, facing traps and nature.
* **Revelation scenes:** pivotal scenes where the course of the story shifts, often linked to the fulfillment or transformation of a Destiny (Lachesis).

### A. Examples of action scenes
**The scuffle in the alleys:** The heroes are ambushed by bandits. The stakes are clear: survive and route the attackers. During this scene, the dice roll at every clash of arms (*Cut the Thread*). It is a scene of rapid attrition where characters risk seeing their traits temporarily degrade or accumulating *Frayed Threads* under the opponents' blows.

**Escaping the burning complex:** The countdown is started via a Timed Urgency Clock (*Building collapse*). The heroes must *Tighten the Thread* to climb, force doors, or avoid debris. Each success with a cost advances the disaster clock while depleting the characters' physical capabilities.

### B. Examples of investigation scenes
**The clandestine autopsy:** In the municipal morgue, a medium and a medical examiner examine a body bearing occult symbols. The stakes are to understand the cause of death before security arrives. The players use the *Read the Pattern* maneuver. A moderate roll (Success with a cost) will uncover the secret, but at the cost of a complication: the night watchman makes his rounds earlier, initiating a transition to an action or interaction scene.

**Searching the forbidden library:** The heroes are looking for a specific ritual among thousands of grimoires. The Game Master manages the scene with a progress clock (*Find the ritual*). The scholars combine their abilities and resources (like old search indexes) to accumulate successes before fatigue (narrative penalties) sets in.

### C. Examples of interaction scenes
**Negotiating with the underworld boss:** The heroes must convince a local gang leader to lend them a hand. The atmosphere is heavy; the NPC is evaluated as *Distrustful*. The player uses their *Diplomat* trait and decides to invoke Clotho's Thread (their Conviction that the city must be saved): they spend a Loom token to add +1d12 to their roll to obtain a total success and shift the boss to a *Friendly* state.

**Interrogating a suspect:** The heroes attempt to make a captured cultist talk. If they choose to use psychological violence or torture, the Game Master can suggest they invoke Atropos's Thread (their Sin): they regain a Loom token, but add +1d4 to their roll, risking a dramatic *Snag* (the suspect dies of a heart attack, or locks themselves in absolute silence).

### D. Examples of rest scenes
**The makeshift clinic:** After a violent clash, the heroes take refuge with an allied doctor. Thorne has a *Taut Thread* (serious injury). The scene is entirely dedicated to his recovery. The doctor uses their equipment (Resource) to optimize the treatment. It is a moment of dialogue and narrative pause where the fiction of the healing (stitches, rest) legitimizes mechanically clearing the deep injury boxes.

**The spiritual sanctuary:** A mage whose mental health is *Thread at Breaking Point* (mortal injury) isolates themselves in a temple to meditate. To heal, they must anchor themselves in their Conviction. The scene explores their doubts and beliefs. If they succeed, they erase their psychological aftereffects before being able to tackle the rest of the adventure.

### E. Examples of flashback scenes
**The memory of the great war:** As a hero is about to give up in the face of an insurmountable obstacle, the player asks for a flashback scene. We then play out a brief moment from their past where their Conviction was born. This memory narratively justifies why the character finds the strength to burn a Thread (*Burnt Thread*) or use a Loom token in the present.

**The past betrayal:** To explain why the character has the Behavior trait *Distrustful of authority*, a flashback scene shows the moment in their life when a mentor abandoned them. This type of scene allows modifying or renaming a trait (*Re-weaving the Fabric*) in full consistency with the hero's personal history.

### F. Examples of exploration scenes
**Crossing the Ash Desert:** The heroes must cross a hostile expanse to reach a ruined city. The Game Master manages this crossing with a progress clock (*Reach the city*). The characters use maneuvers like *Tighten the Thread* to survive the toxic storms or *Read the Pattern* to orient themselves. Failure or success with a cost results in the loss of Resources (supplies run out) or the accumulation of fatigue (checking *Frayed Thread* boxes).

**Opening the Sealed Tomb:** The group arrives in front of a massive, ancient mechanical door. There are no enemies; the obstacle is the location itself. The heroes must use their erudition or mechanics traits, and potentially spend a Resource like a "Fragmented expedition journal" (to double a die). The scene focuses on atmosphere, puzzle-solving, and caution against millennia-old traps.

### G. Examples of revelation scenes
**The fulfillment of Destiny:** After months of searching, the knight whose *Thread of Lachesis* was "Find the rightful heir" realizes that the orphan he has been protecting since the beginning of the adventure bears the royal mark. The scene does not necessarily require dice rolls; it is a moment of pure storytelling where the table recognizes that an *Arc node* has just been reached. The hero sees his Destiny fulfilled, which will allow him to increase a trait at the end of the session.

**The Turning Point of the campaign (The mask falls):** The investigators discover irrefutable evidence in a safe: their wealthy patron is actually the leader of the cult they are hunting. It is a scene of brutal revelation that marks the crossing of the mid-campaign threshold (The Turning Point). The players must reevaluate their alliances, and some may even use the *Re-weaving the Fabric* option to modify one of their Behavior traits, illustrating their loss of innocence in the face of this betrayal.
