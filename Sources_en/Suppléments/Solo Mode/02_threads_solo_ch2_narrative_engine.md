# II. THE NARRATIVE ENGINE

In solo, the Narrative Engine replaces the Game Master. It does not narrate for you — it *answers* your questions, *reacts* to your actions, and *restarts* scenes when inspiration is lacking. It is a tool of resistance against the temptation to always choose what benefits your hero.

The Engine relies on three mechanisms: the Oracle of Fate for closed questions, the Open Question for complex situations, and the Tables from chapter VI for inspiration and content generation.

---

## 1. THE ORACLE OF FATE

When you ask a closed question to the universe — a question whose answer is *Yes* or *No* — roll 1d12 and read the result.

| Result | Answer | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 – 4 | **NO** | The event does not occur, or the information is negative. |
| 5 – 8 | **YES, BUT…** | It happens, but with a nuance, a delay, or a complication. |
| 9 – 12 | **YES** | The answer is positive, the event unfolds in your favor. |

---

### Adjusting probability

Not all questions are equal. Some are unlikely by nature — *"Does the guard fall asleep at his post?"* — others are nearly certain — *"Is the tavern open at midday?"* Before rolling the die, assess the likelihood of a positive answer and adjust the threshold accordingly.

| Likelihood | YES threshold | YES, BUT… threshold | NO threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| Unlikely | 11 – 12 | 7 – 10 | 1 – 6 |
| Uncertain *(default)* | 9 – 12 | 5 – 8 | 1 – 4 |
| Likely | 6 – 12 | 4 – 5 | 1 – 3 |
| Nearly certain | 3 – 12 | 2 | 1 |

> *Example: Your hero is looking for an informant in a port they know well. The question "Do they find someone who knows something?" is **likely** — roll with the corresponding threshold.*

---

### Interpreting YES, BUT…

*Yes, but…* is the richest answer from the Engine — and the most delicate to interpret. Here are a few ways to read it depending on the context:

- **A delay** — it happens, but not now. The answer arrives too late, at the wrong time.
- **A cost** — it happens, but something is lost or sacrificed along the way.
- **A condition** — it happens, but only if the hero accepts a narrative constraint.
- **A complication** — it happens, but a problem arises in parallel.

Choose the interpretation that best serves the promise of your campaign (chapter I). If none comes naturally, consult **Table F — Complications** (chapter VI).

---

### When not to consult the Oracle

The Oracle is a tool, not a crutch. You do not need to consult it for every detail of the world. If the answer is obvious — fictionally, logically, or thematically — trust your judgment and move on.

Consult the Oracle when the answer matters to you *and* when you genuinely do not know what the world should answer. If you already have a strong preference, ask yourself: is this the preference of your hero, or yours? If it is yours, roll the die.

---

## 2. THE OPEN QUESTION

Some situations are too complex for a closed question. *"What is happening in this city?"* or *"What is this NPC's true motivation?"* cannot be resolved by a simple Yes or No.

In these cases, break down the situation into several chained closed questions, or consult the inspiration tables in chapter VI.

**Procedure:**

1. Identify the central element of your question. Reformulate it as a main closed question.
2. Roll the Oracle for this question.
3. Based on the result, ask one or two additional precision questions.
4. Assemble the answers into a coherent picture.

> *Example: "What is the nature of the threat hanging over this village?"*
> - *Question 1: "Is the threat of human origin?" → NO*
> - *Question 2: "Is it linked to the nearby forest?" → YES, BUT…*
> - *Question 3: "Is there a connection to the village's past?" → YES*
> - *Assembled picture: a creature from the forest, drawn by something the villagers did long ago and refuse to admit.*

If the chained answers remain too vague, consult **Table A — Action & Object** or **Table C — Plot Twist** (chapter VI) to inject a concrete element that unblocks the situation.

---

## 3. NPC ATTITUDE

When you encounter an NPC whose disposition is not established by context, roll 1d12 to determine their initial attitude.

| Result | Attitude | What it means in play |
|---|---|---|
| 1 – 2 | **Hostile** | Actively seeks to harm or drive you away. |
| 3 – 5 | **Suspicious** | Uncooperative. Demands proof, guarantees, or compensation. |
| 6 – 7 | **Neutral** | Indifferent. Deals with you out of necessity, without personal investment. |
| 8 – 10 | **Friendly** | Open to discussion. Willing to help within reasonable limits. |
| 11 – 12 | **Allied** | Very favorable. May offer a Resource, valuable information, or spontaneous help. |

### Attitude evolves

The initial attitude is not fixed. It reflects the first impression — what the NPC feels *before* interacting with your hero. Your actions, traits, and dice rolls can shift it in either direction.

Note each significant NPC in your Thread Lexicon (chapter V) with their current attitude. An attitude that changes is a narrative signal — something important has just been woven.

To quickly generate an NPC beyond their attitude, consult **Table D — Improvised NPC** (chapter VI).

## 4. RESTARTING A STALLING SCENE

Sometimes, the story stalls. You do not know what to do with your hero, the Oracle answers *Yes, but…* without anything imposing itself, and the fiction goes in circles. This is normal — it happens even to the best Game Masters.

When this occurs, in order:

**Step 1 — Consult your Threads.** Reread your three Threads. Does an action in accord with your Conviction impose itself? Does your Destiny indicate a direction? Does your Sin pull you toward something you are avoiding? Often, the answer is already on your character sheet.

**Step 2 — Consult Table C.** **Table C — Plot Twist** (chapter VI) is designed for exactly this moment. It injects an external event that changes the parameters of the scene without asking you to invent it.

**Step 3 — Close the scene.** If nothing works, consult **Table H — Scene Endings** (chapter VI) to find how to properly close this scene and open a new one. A scene that ends is always preferable to a scene that spins its wheels.
