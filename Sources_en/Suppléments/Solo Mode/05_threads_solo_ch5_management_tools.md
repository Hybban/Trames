# V. MANAGEMENT TOOLS

Solo play stretches over time. Between two sessions, the fiction cools down, details fade, and threads get lost. This chapter proposes three simple tools to keep your campaign alive and readable from one session to the next.

These tools are not obligations — they are habits. Adopt those that suit you, ignore the rest.

---

## 1. THE THREAD JOURNAL

The Thread Journal is your campaign memory. Its principle is minimalist: **one sentence per scene.**

Not a detailed summary. Not a literary description. One sentence that captures the essential — what happened, what it changed, or what it revealed.

> *Examples:*
> *— "Found the informant at the port, but he was already under surveillance."*
> *— "Invoked Atropos to intimidate the merchant — it worked, but someone saw."*
> *— "First Arc threshold: the rival faction comes into play."*

### Suggested structure for each session

At the start of a session, reread the last five entries of your journal before rolling the first die. No more — the goal is to find the thread again, not to reread everything.

At the end of a session, note:

1. **The scenes played** — one sentence per scene, in order.
2. **The state of the clocks** — Narrative Arc, Clock of Sin, and any active external force clock.
3. **The open question** — a single question left unanswered, which will serve as the starting point for the next session.

The open question is the most important element. It transforms the end of a session into the beginning of a session. When you reopen your journal two weeks later, you know exactly where to pick up.

> *Examples of open questions:*
> *— "Who ordered the ambush?"*
> *— "Does the ally know that I lied to her?"*
> *— "Is the threat to the north linked to what I discovered in the archives?"*

## 2. THE THREAD LEXICON

The Thread Lexicon is a living list of all significant NPCs encountered throughout the campaign. It replaces the Game Master's memory — in solo, nobody else remembers who was who.

### Structure of an entry

Each entry fits in three lines maximum:

```
NAME — Role or function in the story
Current attitude: [Hostile / Suspicious / Neutral / Friendly / Allied]
Note: what this person knows, wants, or hides.
```

> *Example:*
> ```
> SERA — Archivist of the Council, keeper of the ancient records
> Current attitude: Suspicious
> Note: knows something about the disappearance of the former Archetype. Will not talk without guarantees.
> ```

### Keeping the Lexicon up to date

After each scene involving an NPC, update their entry if their attitude has changed or if new information has emerged. An NPC who shifts from Suspicious to Friendly after a key scene deserves to be noted — it is a thread that has just changed color.

Do not list every NPC encountered. Only those likely to reappear or to influence the story deserve an entry. Extras remain extras.

---

## 3. CLOCKS IN VIEW

Clocks are the most visible management tool in THREADS. In solo, they fulfill an additional role: they remind you at all times where your story stands.

### Suggested layout

Keep all your active clocks on a separate sheet, or in a dedicated corner of your play space. The layout matters little — what matters is that they are visible without effort.

A readable organization:

```
[ NARRATIVE ARC           ] ○ ○ ○ ● ● ○ ○ ○ ○ ○   (4/10)
[ CLOCK OF SIN            ] ○ ● ● ○ ○ ○             (2/6)
[ Force: The Guild        ] ● ● ● ● ○ ○             (4/6)
[ Objective: The Codex    ] ● ○ ○ ○                 (1/4)
```

### Reading clocks together

Clocks are not read one by one — they are read in relation to each other. Before each session, look at the whole and ask yourself two questions:

*"Which clock is closest to being full?"*
This is your current narrative priority. The story must address it, whether your hero wants to or not.

*"Is there a tension between two clocks?"*
If the Narrative Arc is at the halfway point and the Clock of Sin is already at three-quarters, something is going wrong. This is dramatic information — use it.

### Creating and closing clocks

Do not hesitate to create new short clocks mid-campaign whenever a threat or objective justifies it. And close completed clocks — cross them out, or note their conclusion. A closed clock is a permanent narrative fact. It is part of the story.

---

## A word on regularity

These three tools work best if you play with some regularity. One session per week, even a short one, is better than a long monthly session followed by three weeks of silence. The thread holds when you hold it often.

If a long interruption is unavoidable, reread your entire journal before resuming, not just the last five entries. And consult the Oracle with the question: *"Has something changed during my absence?"* The world does not stop when you put down the dice.
