# III. CAMPAIGN STRUCTURES

The SRD proposes short clocks — 4 to 6 segments — to represent immediate threats or scene objectives. In solo, a campaign needs longer structures that provide depth, direction, and a sense of progression across multiple sessions.

This chapter proposes three complementary tools: the Narrative Arc to track the progression of Destiny, the Clock of Sin to give weight to Atropos invocations, and Crossed Threads to manage factions and opposing forces.

---

## 1. THE NARRATIVE ARC

The Narrative Arc is a long clock of **8 to 12 segments** that represents your hero's progression toward the fulfillment of their Destiny — their Lachesis Thread. It is drawn at the beginning of the campaign and remains visible at all times.

### Calibrating the length

The length of the Arc depends on the ambition of your campaign.

| Length | Estimated sessions | Suited for |
|---|---|---|
| 8 segments | 4 to 6 sessions | A short story, a single arc |
| 10 segments | 6 to 10 sessions | A medium-sized campaign |
| 12 segments | 10 sessions and more | A long epic, a complex destiny |

If you do not yet know what duration you are aiming for, start at 8. It is always possible to extend the Arc mid-campaign if the story stretches — the Moirai sometimes have more to say than expected.

### Progressing through the Arc

A segment is ticked when an **arc knot** is reached — as defined in the SRD (section V). Reminder: an arc knot is recognized when one of the three Threads has been honored, tested, or pulled in a significant way.

In solo, it is you who evaluates whether a knot has been reached. Ask yourself honestly after each important scene: *"Has something changed in who my hero is, or in the direction their story is taking?"* If yes, tick a segment. If you are unsure, consult the Oracle — *"Does this moment deserve an arc knot?"*

### Arc Thresholds

At certain points on the clock, structural events trigger. These thresholds give rhythm to the campaign and prevent the Arc from being a simple list of boxes to tick.

**At the quarter mark of the Arc:** The world reacts. An opposing force comes into play — a faction, a rival, a threat that your actions have awakened. Create a short clock (4 to 6 segments) for this force and start advancing it.

**At the halfway mark of the Arc:** A turning point. Something your hero believed to be true turns out to be false, incomplete, or more complex than expected. Their Lachesis Thread does not disappear — but it changes shape. Reformulate the Destiny in one sentence if necessary.

**At the three-quarter mark of the Arc:** The price becomes visible. The campaign enters its final phase. The stakes are clear, resources depleted, and Threads stretched to the breaking point. It is often here that the Burnt Thread becomes a real possibility.

**At the last segment:** The campaign ending opens. Consult section 4 of this chapter.

## 2. THE CLOCK OF SIN

The Clock of Sin is a clock running parallel to the Narrative Arc. It fills each time Atropos is invoked — each time your hero lets their Sin guide their hand.

### Calibrating the Clock of Sin

At the creation of the campaign, choose the size of the Clock of Sin according to the relationship you want to maintain with your hero's vice.

| Size | What it means |
|---|---|
| 4 segments | Sin is a constant threat. Atropos is close. |
| 6 segments | The standard balance. Sin weighs heavy, but leaves some margin. |
| 8 segments | Sin is a slow temptation. The fall is a long time coming. |

### Progressing through the Clock of Sin

A segment is ticked **with each invocation of Atropos** — each time you use Atropos's Thread to regain a token from the Loom.

Note that ticking a segment is not a punishment. It is a signal that something is being woven in the shadow of your hero. The Clock of Sin does not punish weakness — it gives it weight.

### When the Clock of Sin is full

Define **before the start of the campaign** what happens when the Clock of Sin fills. It is not necessarily a catastrophe — but it is always a major and irreversible consequence, consistent with the nature of your hero's Sin.

> *Examples:*
> *— A hero whose Sin is "anger" loses control publicly and destroys a precious alliance.*
> *— A hero whose Sin is "greed" finds themselves betrayed by someone they themselves corrupted.*
> *— A hero whose Sin is "deception" sees their true identity exposed at the worst moment.*

Once the consequence is triggered, reset the Clock of Sin to zero. Atropos has cut, but the thread continues — damaged, but present. The consequence remains in the fiction.

### The interaction between the two clocks

The Narrative Arc and the Clock of Sin progress in parallel, but in opposite directions. One measures your ascent, the other your corrosion. The tension between the two is the dramatic heart of your campaign.

If the Clock of Sin fills *before* the Narrative Arc reaches its midpoint threshold, the campaign has tipped earlier than expected — and this is valuable narrative information. Your hero may be more fragile than they thought.

## 3. CROSSED THREADS

Crossed Threads allow you to manage forces external to the hero — rival factions, active threats, allies pursuing their own objectives. In solo, these forces need structures to exist independently of your actions.

### Creating an external force

Each significant external force is represented by:

- **A name and an intention** — what this force wants to accomplish.
- **A clock** — 4, 6, or 8 segments depending on the scope of the threat.
- **A progression rhythm** — when the clock advances.

The progression rhythm is key. An external force should not progress only in reaction to your failures — it has its own logic. Define a simple condition that advances its clock, independently of your rolls.

> *Examples of rhythms:*
> *— "The clock advances by one segment at the end of each session where I have not directly countered this force."*
> *— "The clock advances by one segment each time I consult the Oracle and get a NO."*
> *— "The clock advances by one segment at each Narrative Arc threshold."*

### Making forces interact

When two external forces have opposing objectives, they can clash indirectly while your hero pursues something else. In this case, roll 1d12 at the end of each session to determine which one progresses.

| Result | Effect |
|---|---|
| 1 – 4 | The force most hostile to your hero advances by one segment. |
| 5 – 8 | Neither progresses — they cancel each other out. |
| 9 – 12 | The force most favorable to your hero advances by one segment. |

Do not overpopulate your campaign. Two or three active external forces simultaneously is a comfortable maximum. Beyond that, managing clocks becomes an end in itself rather than a narrative tool.

## 4. THE CAMPAIGN ENDING

When the Narrative Arc reaches its last segment, the campaign enters its conclusion. There is not a single way to end — the Moirai have woven three possible types of endings.

### Destiny Fulfilled
*Lachesis has measured — the thread was exactly the right length.*

The hero has reached what they were destined for. Their Lachesis Thread has been fulfilled, in its original form or in the form it took at the midpoint turning. It is an ending of victory — but not necessarily a happy ending. A fulfilled destiny can be costly.

Mechanically, the campaign ends after the concluding scene. The player can choose to start a new campaign with the same hero — their Arc restarts at zero, their Destiny is rewritten, but their traits and their Atropos and Clotho Threads are preserved.

### Sin Victorious
*Atropos has cut — the thread was not what we thought it was.*

The Clock of Sin has filled one time too many, or the hero has made a final choice that places their Sin above everything else. This is not necessarily death — it is transformation. The hero is no longer the same moral character as at the beginning of the campaign.

This ending is valid and can be beautiful. Some of the best stories end this way. If you choose to continue with this hero, their Atropos Thread must be rewritten to reflect what they have become — and their Conviction (Clotho) will have to answer for this change.

### The Final Burnt Thread
*Clotho has spun to the very end — even when there was nothing left to give.*

The hero has survived the impossible by sacrificing a part of themselves. This ending activates the Burnt Thread rule (chapter IV) one last time, in the context of a campaign ending. What is burned here is definitive — not just for the scene, but for the entire story.

This is the most dramatically charged ending. It requires that the player accept that their hero has come out of the adventure permanently and irreversibly changed. The Moirai have demanded the maximum price — and it has been paid.
