# II. Resolution Mechanics

The TRAMES system is based on rolling a pool of dice and directly using the highest result. This system handles success, failure, and the wear and tear on your abilities through physical damage to the dice.

## 1. The Die Roll
When an action is uncertain or risky, you must perform a test:

* **Selection:** Choose between **1 and 3 traits** relevant to the action. 
* **Roll:** Roll the dice corresponding to the current value of these traits (d6, d8, d10, or d12).
* **Result:** Keep only the value of the **highest die**.

## 2. Interpretation and Consequences
The score obtained on the die you kept determines the outcome of the action and how your traits change for the rest of the scene:
| Result | Outcome | Effect on Traits |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **9 – 12** | **Total Success** | The action is a complete success. **Increase** the value of one of the traits used by one level. |
| **5 – 8** | **Success with a Price** | The action succeeds, but with a consequence. **Decrease** the value of one of the traits used by one level. |
| **1 – 4** | **Failure** | The action fails. **Decrease** the value of one of the traits used by one level. |

**Notes:**

* A trait cannot exceed its maximum value. 
* A trait cannot normally drop below d6. 

If you have no choice but to reduce a trait that is already at its minimum, that trait can no longer be used for the rest of the scene. 

**Note:** Be careful—the more traits you use, the greater your chances of success, but the more you expose yourself to complications.

> **The "1" Rule (The Snag):** Regardless of the final result, every "1" rolled on any of the dice creates a snag in the plot. A complication arises: a reduction of one level on an additional die, a resource that becomes inaccessible, or a narrative constraint. The Moirai have pulled a thread of your destiny.

## 3. The Open-Ended Question
The dice determine the heroes’ actions. But some questions the Game Master asks are not actions, but rather questions about the state of the world, an NPC’s intentions, or the direction the story is taking. For these moments, a different procedure is required.

When a question is too complex to be decided directly, break it down into several linked closed questions, each answered with *Yes* or *No*, and roll 1d12 for each.
| Result | Answer |
|---|---|
| 9 – 12 | **Yes** — The element is present, positive, or favorable. |
| 5 – 8 | **Yes, but…** — The element is present, but with a nuance or complication. |
| 1 – 4 | **No** — The element is absent, negative, or unfavorable. |

Combine the answers into a coherent picture that will serve as the basis for the narrative.

**Note:** The Open Question is not a substitute for preparation. It is a tool for moments when the story takes a direction the Game Master did not anticipate and the world must respond coherently without everything being decided in advance.

## 4. Degradation and Exhaustion
The value of a trait fluctuates during the game. Chapter 03 describes the rules for trait health and recovery.

* **The Archetype:** This trait is the core of your identity; it can **never drop below d6** and therefore can never be disabled.
* **Other Traits:** If a trait (Behavior or Ability) drops below d6, it becomes **unusable** at least for the remainder of the scene. It can only be used again after a period of rest or a change in narrative context.
* **Other Cases:** If a trait must drop and you do not want to lower it (or cannot): you may also sacrifice a resource or take a minor injury instead. Similarly, if you need to increase a trait and it reaches its maximum, the player can heal one wound box instead or regain the use of a resource. Use these flexibilities wisely to serve the story.

## 5. The Burnt Thread: The Ultimate Sacrifice
Sometimes, the Fates decide that your story is not over—but they demand a price. The Burnt Thread is the moment when a hero sacrifices a part of themselves so that their thread may continue. It is not a victory. It is a survival that leaves a permanent scar in the character’s fabric.

* **Activation:** The Burnt Thread can only be invoked when faced with a truly fatal outcome—a final, fatal wound, or the irreversible failure of an arc-ending objective.
* **Effect:** The current action is instantly transformed into a Total Success. The danger is averted.
* **The Cost:** Permanently erase one of your traits. It can no longer be used, renamed, or increased. That thread is severed.
* **Note on the Archetype:** Burning one’s Archetype is theoretically possible, but this is its most tragic form. The hero survives, but loses their essence. They will have to rebuild themselves around a new Archetype, which will start at d6.
* **Group Note:** The entire table must feel the weight of this moment. Take the time for the other players to acknowledge what has just been lost—it is a permanent narrative fact, not a mere technical rule.

## 6. The Loom of the Moirai

The “Loom” replaces traditional luck points and was introduced in the previous section. It is a system of exchange between your convictions, your destiny, and your flaws. The three Moirai do not intervene in your story in the same way. Clotho spins—it costs you something, because creation requires effort. Lachesis measures—she aligns with you for free, because Fate cannot be forced; it must be recognized. Atropos cuts—she gives you energy in exchange for a risk, because yielding to your dark instincts invigorates you while fraying your thread.

These three dynamics are not equivalent. They are complementary—and it is this tension that makes the heart of TRAMES beat.

### A. Invoking the Threads
Using the threads allows you to exceed the usual limit of 3 dice per roll.

* **Lachesis’ Thread (Fate):** Once per session, if you act to fulfill your destiny, add **+1d12** to your roll. This use does not cost Craft points, but is limited by its rarity.

* **The Thread of Clotho (Conviction):** If your action is driven by deep conviction, spend **1 token** from the Craft to add **+1d12** to your roll. The token is then given to the GM (or “committed” in a solo session).

* **The Thread of Atropos (Sin):** If you give in to your evil instincts or your vice, you can **regain 1 token** (taken from the GM or "reactivated" in solo play). By invoking Atropos, you let the scissors come close to your thread. Add **+1d4** to your roll. This die cannot help you, but it can complicate everything. That is the price of surrender.

### B. Optional - The Clock of Sin
Every invocation of Atropos leaves a mark. Taken in isolation, it is merely a token regained, a d4 added, a weakness accepted. But the Moirai keep track. And what accumulates always ends up weighing heavily.

At the start of the campaign, the Game Master creates a **Clock of Sin** for each hero—a circle of 4, 6, or 8 segments, calibrated according to the relationship the player wishes to maintain with their character’s vice. The chosen game mode (linen, wool, silk, or gold thread) can also affect the size of this clock.
| Size | What it means |
|---|---|
| 4 segments | Sin is a constant threat. Atropos is near. |
| 6 segments | The standard balance. Sin weighs heavily, but leaves some leeway. |
| 8 segments | Sin is a slow temptation. The fall is a long time coming. |

One segment is checked off each time Atropos is invoked.

**When the clock is full**, a major and irreversible consequence is triggered, defined by the Game Master and the player together at the start of the campaign, consistent with the nature of the hero’s Sin.

Once the consequence is triggered, the clock resets. Atropos has cut the thread, but it continues, damaged. The consequence remains within the story.

> **Note:** The Clock of Sin is not a punishment. It is the materialization of the promise of Atropos’s Thread—that the Sin will cause the hero’s downfall or lead to their ruin. Without this clock, that promise remains mere decorative fiction. With it, it becomes a mechanical reality.
