# II. Resolution Mechanic

The TRAMES system is based on rolling a pool of dice and directly reading the highest result. This system handles success, failure, as well as the wear and tear of your abilities through a physical degradation of the dice.

## 1. The Dice Roll
When an action is uncertain or risky, you must perform a test:

* **Selection:** Choose between **1 and 3 traits** relevant to the action.
* **Roll:** Roll the dice corresponding to the current value of these traits (d6, d8, d10, or d12).
* **Result:** Keep only the value of the **highest die**.

**Note:** The more traits you mobilize, the more you increase your chances of success, but the more you expose yourself to complications.

## 2. Interpretation and Consequences
The score obtained on the kept die determines the outcome of the action and the evolution of your traits for the rest of the scene:

| Result | Outcome | Consequence on Traits |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **9 – 12** | **Total success** | The action is a full success. **Increase** the value of one of the used traits by one step. |
| **5 – 8** | **Success with a cost** | The action succeeds, but with a consequence. **Decrease** the value of only one of the used traits by one step. |
| **1 – 4** | **Failure** | The action fails. **Decrease** the value of one of the used traits by one step. |

**Reminder:** A trait cannot exceed its maximum value. Furthermore, a trait cannot normally fall below a d6.

If you have no choice but to decrease a trait that is already at its minimum, the trait may become impossible to use for the rest of the scene.

> **The Rule of "1" (The Snag):** Regardless of the final result, each "1" rolled on all the dice cast creates a snag in the thread. A complication arises: a decrease of one step on an additional die, a resource becoming inaccessible, or a narrative constraint. The Moirai have pulled a thread awry.

## 3. The Open Question
The dice decide the actions of the heroes. But some questions the Game Master asks are not actions — they concern the state of the world, the intentions of an NPC, or the direction the story is taking. For these moments, another procedure is needed.

When a question is too complex to be decided directly, break it down into several chained closed questions — questions whose answer is *Yes* or *No* — and roll 1d12 for each.

| Result | Answer |
|---|---|
| 1 – 4 | **No** — The element is absent, negative, or unfavorable. |
| 5 – 8 | **Yes, but…** — The element is present, but with a nuance or a complication. |
| 9 – 12 | **Yes** — The element is present, positive, or favorable. |

Assemble the answers into a coherent picture that will serve as the basis for the narration.

**Note:** The Open Question is not a substitute for preparation. It is a tool for the moments when the story takes a direction the Game Master had not anticipated — and when the world must respond coherently without everything being decided in advance.

## 4. Degradation and Exhaustion
The value of a trait fluctuates during play. At the end of a scene, all traits return to their initial value (their maximum defined at creation).

* **The Archetype:** This trait is the core of your identity; it can **never fall below a d6**.
* **Other Traits:** If a trait (Behavior or Ability) must fall below a d6, it becomes **unusable** for the rest of the scene. It cannot be called upon again until after a period of rest or a change in narrative context.
* **Other cases:** If a trait must decrease and you do not want to decrease it (or cannot): you could also sacrifice a resource or take a light wound instead. Similarly, if you must increase a trait and it has reached its maximum, the player can heal one wound box instead. Use these flexibilities wisely to serve the narrative.

## 5. The Burnt Thread: The Ultimate Sacrifice
Sometimes, the Moirai decide that your story is not over — but they demand a price. The Burnt Thread is the moment when a hero sacrifices a part of themselves so that their thread continues. It is not a victory. It is a survival that leaves a permanent scar in the fabric of the character.

* **Activation:** The Burnt Thread can only be invoked when facing a truly fatal outcome — a final mortal wound, or the irreversible failure of an end-of-arc objective.
* **The Effect:** The current action is instantly transformed into a Total Success. The danger is averted.
* **The Price:** Permanently erase one of your traits. It can no longer be used, renamed, or increased. This thread is cut.
* **Note on Archetype:** Burning one's Archetype is possible, but it is the most tragic form. The hero survives, but loses their essence. They must rebuild themselves around a new Archetype, which starts at d6.
* **Group Note:** The entire table must feel the weight of this moment. Take the time for the other players to acknowledge what has just been lost — it is a permanent narrative fact, not just a technical rule.

## 6. The Loom of the Fates

The "Loom" replaces classic luck points and was presented in the previous section. It is a system of exchange between your convictions, your destiny, and your flaws. The three Moirai do not intervene in the same way in your story. Clotho spins — she costs you something, because creating requires effort. Lachesis measures — she aligns with you for free, because Destiny cannot be forced, it is recognized. Atropos cuts — she gives you energy in exchange for a risk, because giving in to your dark instincts invigorates you while fraying your thread.

These three dynamics are not equivalent. They are complementary — and it is this tension that makes the heart of THREADS beat.

### A. Invoking the Threads
Using the threads allows you to exceed the usual limit of 3 dice per roll.

* **Lachesis's Thread (Destiny):** Once per session, if you act to fulfill your destiny, add **+1d12** to your roll. This use does not cost a Loom point, but is limited by its rarity.

* **Clotho's Thread (Conviction):** If your action is driven by your deep conviction, spend **1 token** from the Loom to add **+1d12** to your roll. The token is then given to the GM (or "committed" in solo).

* **Atropos's Thread (Sin):** If you give in to your bad instincts or your vice, you can **regain 1 token** (taken from the GM or "reactivated" in solo). By invoking Atropos, you let the scissors approach your thread. Add **+1d4** to your roll. This die cannot help you, but it can complicate everything. It is the price of surrender.

### B. The Clock of Sin
Every invocation of Atropos leaves a mark. Taken in isolation, it is just a regained token, a d4 added, an accepted weakness. But the Moirai keep count. And what accumulates always ends up weighing heavy.

At the beginning of the campaign, the Game Master creates a **Clock of Sin** for each hero — a circle of 4, 6, or 8 segments, calibrated according to the relationship the player wants to maintain with their character's vice.

| Size | What it means |
|---|---|
| 4 segments | Sin is a constant threat. Atropos is close. |
| 6 segments | The standard balance. Sin weighs heavy, but leaves some margin. |
| 8 segments | Sin is a slow temptation. The fall is a long time coming. |

One segment is ticked with each invocation of Atropos.

**When the clock is full**, a major and irreversible consequence triggers — defined by the Game Master and the player together at the start of the campaign, consistent with the nature of the hero's Sin.

Once the consequence is triggered, the clock is reset to zero. Atropos has cut — but the thread continues, damaged. The consequence remains in the fiction.

> **Note:** The Clock of Sin is not a punishment. It is the materialization of the promise of Atropos's Thread — that Sin will cause the downfall of the hero, or make them fall. Without this clock, this promise remains decorative fiction. With it, it becomes mechanical.
